## How This Analysis Works

### Liquid Chromatography–Mass Spectrometry (LC-MS/MS)

Think of LC-MS/MS as a sophisticated molecular identification system. First, proteins from your cells are chopped up into smaller pieces called peptides using enzymes (like using molecular scissors). These peptides are then separated by liquid chromatography, which works like sorting mail - peptides with different chemical properties (more "sticky" or hydrophobic vs. more water-loving) travel through a column at different speeds, spreading them out in time.

After separation, peptides enter the mass spectrometer where they're given an electrical charge (ionized) and measured. The mass spectrometer does two key things: first, it measures the mass of whole peptides (MS1), like weighing intact letters. Then it breaks these peptides into fragments and measures those pieces (MS2), like opening the letters to read the contents. By knowing both the weight of the whole peptide and the pattern of fragments, sophisticated software can identify which protein each peptide came from and how much of that protein was present.

### Data-Independent Acquisition (DIA)

Traditional mass spectrometry (called DDA - Data-Dependent Acquisition) works like having one very attentive but limited postal worker who can only examine the loudest, most obvious packages one at a time. If five packages arrive simultaneously, they'll pick the biggest/loudest one and ignore the others until later, potentially missing many smaller packages entirely.

DIA is like having a systematic quality control system that examines *everything* in predetermined windows. Instead of cherry-picking the most abundant peptides, DIA systematically scans across the entire mass range in windows, ensuring that every peptide gets measured every single time. This means rare proteins don't get overlooked just because abundant proteins are present, giving you more complete and reproducible data.

The tradeoff is that DIA data is much more complex - like recording every conversation in a crowded room simultaneously rather than listening to one person at a time. You need specialized software to untangle all these overlapping signals.

### DIA-NN Software

DIA-NN is the detective that makes sense of the complex DIA data puzzle. Imagine you recorded every conversation at a party simultaneously - DIA-NN figures out who said what by comparing the recording to what it expects each person's voice to sound like.

DIA-NN uses neural networks (AI) to score how confident it is that a particular signal represents a specific peptide. Its key innovation is "library-free" analysis - instead of requiring a pre-built database of what peptides should look like, it predicts what peptides from any protein should look like based on their sequence and physics. This is like being able to recognize someone's voice even if you've never heard them speak before, just by knowing the shape of their vocal cords.

The software outputs a report telling you which proteins were present in each sample and their relative quantities - essentially a comprehensive molecular census of your cells.

### LIMPA / limma Statistical Framework

Once you have protein quantities, you face a statistics problem: which differences between groups are real biology versus random measurement noise? This is especially challenging in proteomics because you're testing thousands of proteins simultaneously with typically few biological replicates.

Limma's breakthrough insight is "borrowing strength across genes" using empirical Bayes moderation. Here's the intuition: if you want to know how variable one specific protein is, looking at just that protein's measurements might not give you enough information. But if you look at how variable *all* the proteins are in your experiment, you can get a much better estimate.

It's like estimating how much traffic varies on one specific street. If you only have 3 days of data for that street, your estimate might be poor. But if you know how traffic varies on hundreds of similar streets in the same city, you can make a much better prediction about your specific street by combining your limited data with this broader knowledge.

LIMPA (Linear Models for Mass Spectrometry Proteomics and Metabolomics) is an R package that wraps limma with proteomics-specific preprocessing steps like normalization and filtering, making it easy to apply this powerful statistical framework to protein data.

### Key Statistical Concepts

**log2 Fold Change (logFC)**: This measures how much a protein goes up or down between groups on a logarithmic scale. A logFC of +1 means the protein doubled (2x higher), -1 means it halved (2x lower), and +2 means it quadrupled (4x higher). We use log scale because biological changes are often multiplicative rather than additive - a protein might go from 10 to 100 units rather than 10 to 20 units.

**P-value**: The probability of seeing a difference this large (or larger) if there were actually no real difference between groups - essentially, "how likely is this to be just random noise?" A p-value of 0.01 means there's only a 1% chance this difference occurred by random chance alone.

**Adjusted P-value (FDR)**: When testing thousands of proteins simultaneously, some will appear significant by chance alone - like flipping 1,000 coins and expecting some to come up heads 8 times out of 10 just by luck. The Benjamini-Hochberg correction adjusts p-values to control the "false discovery rate" - the proportion of your "significant" results that are actually false positives.

**Volcano plot**: Gets its name because it's shaped like a volcano when you plot fold-change (x-axis) versus significance (y-axis). The "crater" at the top contains the most significant proteins, while proteins with large fold-changes but low significance form the "slopes." Points far from the origin in both dimensions are your most interesting candidates.

**Coefficient of Variation (CV)**: Measures reproducibility by dividing the standard deviation by the mean. Lower CV means more consistent measurements across replicates. A protein with CV=5% varies much less between replicates than one with CV=50%, making it a more reliable biomarker.

**Normalization**: Raw protein intensities need correction because samples might have different total amounts loaded, different ionization efficiency, or systematic biases. Cyclic loess normalization works like this: imagine you're comparing test scores across different schools, but some schools made their tests easier. You'd normalize by comparing each school to the average of all schools, then adjust iteratively until all schools have comparable distributions. This removes technical bias while preserving biological differences.

## Methodology & Reproducibility

### Statistical Analysis Pipeline

Protein quantification data from DIA-NN was processed using the LIMPA framework in R, implementing the following key steps:

1. **Quality Control**: Sample-wise assessment of precursor and protein identification rates, with MS1 signal intensity evaluation for outlier detection.

2. **Normalization**: Cyclic loess normalization was applied to correct for systematic technical variation while preserving biological differences between groups.

3. **Statistical Testing**: Linear modeling using the limma package with empirical Bayes moderation for variance estimation. This approach is particularly powerful for proteomics data with limited replicates, as it borrows information across all proteins to improve statistical inference.

4. **Multiple Testing Correction**: Benjamini-Hochberg false discovery rate (FDR) correction was applied to control for multiple hypothesis testing across 4,854 proteins.

### Software Versions and Parameters
- DIA-NN: Neural network-based peptide identification with library-free search
- R statistical environment with limma/LIMPA packages
- Significance threshold: adjusted p-value < 0.05

## Overview

This analysis examined **6 comparisons** across **4,854 proteins** using a comprehensive DIA-NN/limma pipeline. The experimental design compared four sample preparation methods: Affinisep, Evosep, affinisepACN, and affinisepIPA, each with 3 biological replicates (n=12 total samples).

**Significant Protein Summary:**
- **affinisepACN vs Affinisep**: 1 significant (1 up, 0 down)
- **affinisepIPA vs Affinisep**: 0 significant
- **Evosep vs Affinisep**: 1,476 significant (23 up, 1,453 down) 
- **affinisepIPA vs affinisepACN**: 1 significant (0 up, 1 down)
- **Evosep vs affinisepACN**: 2,024 significant (24 up, 2,000 down)
- **Evosep vs affinisepIPA**: 326 significant (21 up, 305 down)

The Evosep method shows dramatically different protein expression profiles compared to all Affinisep-based methods, suggesting fundamentally different sample preparation characteristics affecting protein recovery and quantification.

## How This Analysis Works

### Liquid Chromatography–Mass Spectrometry (LC-MS/MS)

Think of LC-MS/MS as a sophisticated identification and counting system for proteins. First, proteins are chopped up into smaller pieces called peptides using digestive enzymes - imagine cutting a book into individual sentences to make them easier to analyze. These peptides are then separated by liquid chromatography, which works like a microscopic obstacle course where different peptides stick to the column material for different amounts of time based on their chemical properties (hydrophobic peptides stick longer, hydrophilic ones flow through faster).

After separation, peptides enter the mass spectrometer where they're ionized (given an electric charge) and measured in two ways: first, the intact peptide mass is measured (MS1), then the peptide is broken into fragments and those fragments are measured (MS2). This is like weighing a whole car, then smashing it and weighing all the pieces - the pattern of fragments acts like a fingerprint that identifies which peptide (and therefore which protein) you're looking at.

### Data-Independent Acquisition (DIA)

Traditional mass spectrometry (DDA - Data-Dependent Acquisition) is like having a conversation in a noisy room where you can only listen to the loudest person speaking at any given time. You might miss quieter but important conversations happening simultaneously.

DIA is like having superhuman hearing that systematically scans the entire room, capturing every conversation happening across all volume levels. Instead of picking just the most abundant peptides to analyze, DIA divides the entire mass range into systematic windows and measures everything in each window. This means every peptide gets measured every time, not just the loudest ones. The trade-off is that the data becomes much more complex - imagine trying to untangle dozens of overlapping conversations recorded simultaneously.

### DIA-NN Software

DIA-NN is the artificial intelligence that makes sense of this complex overlapping data. Think of it as a sophisticated audio engineer who can take a recording of 50 people talking at once and separate it back into individual conversations, identifying who said what.

The software uses neural networks (machine learning algorithms) to score how confident it is in each peptide identification. Importantly, DIA-NN performs "library-free" searches, meaning it doesn't need a pre-built reference library of what peptides should look like. Instead, it predicts what peptides should look like based on the protein sequence and compares these predictions to what it actually observes. This is like having a translator who can figure out a new language by pattern recognition rather than needing a dictionary.

The final output is a report showing how much of each protein was detected in each sample.

### LIMPA / limma Statistical Framework

Once you have protein quantities, you need statistics to separate real biological differences from random noise - like distinguishing genuine height differences between groups from measurement errors.

The key innovation of limma is **information borrowing across proteins**. In traditional statistics, you'd analyze each protein independently, but limma recognizes that all proteins in a sample share similar technical variability. It's like having thousands of thermometers in the same room - if most show consistent readings, you can be more confident about the accuracy of each individual measurement.

This "empirical Bayes moderation" works by pooling variance estimates across all proteins to get better statistics for each individual protein. This is especially powerful in proteomics where you often have few biological replicates (expensive experiments) but thousands of proteins providing information about technical variability.

LIMPA is an R package that wraps limma with proteomics-specific preprocessing steps like normalization (correcting for different amounts of sample loaded) and filtering (removing unreliable measurements).

### Key Statistical Concepts

**log2 Fold Change (logFC)**: Measures how much a protein increases or decreases between conditions on a logarithmic scale. A logFC of +1 means the protein doubled, -1 means it halved, +2 means it quadrupled. We use log scale because biological changes are often multiplicative (2x, 4x, 8x) rather than additive (+1, +2, +3).

**P-value**: The probability of seeing this large a difference by random chance alone. A p-value of 0.01 means there's only a 1% chance this difference occurred randomly.

**Adjusted P-value (FDR)**: When testing thousands of proteins simultaneously, some will appear significant by chance alone - like flipping 1000 coins and finding some that come up heads 8 times out of 10. The False Discovery Rate correction (Benjamini-Hochberg) adjusts p-values to account for this multiple testing problem, controlling the expected proportion of false discoveries.

**Volcano plot**: Shaped like a volcano because most proteins cluster near the center (small changes, not significant) while truly significant proteins appear in the "wings" (large effect size AND high significance). The x-axis shows effect size (logFC), y-axis shows significance (-log10 p-value).

**Coefficient of Variation (CV)**: Standard deviation divided by the mean, expressed as a percentage. It measures measurement reproducibility - a protein with 5% CV varies less between replicates than one with 20% CV.

**Normalization**: Raw mass spectrometer signals vary due to technical factors like slightly different sample amounts loaded. Cyclic loess normalization works by comparing each sample to every other sample in a pairwise manner, applying smooth corrections to make the overall intensity distributions similar while preserving biological differences.

## Methodology & Reproducibility

### Data Processing Pipeline

Raw DIA-MS data were processed using DIA-NN (version specified in `Methods_and_References.txt`) with library-free search parameters optimized for HeLa digest samples. Protein quantification employed the MaxLFQ algorithm with match-between-runs enabled across technical replicates.

### Statistical Analysis

Differential expression analysis was performed using the DE-LIMP (Differential Expression - LIMPA Pipeline) R package, implementing the following workflow:

1. **Quality Control**: Sample-level metrics evaluated for precursor counts, protein identifications, and MS1 signal intensity
2. **Normalization**: Cyclic loess normalization applied to log2-transformed protein intensities
3. **Statistical Testing**: Linear modeling with empirical Bayes moderation via limma package
4. **Multiple Testing Correction**: Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction applied across all proteins per comparison
5. **Significance Thresholds**: FDR < 0.05 considered statistically significant

### Reproducibility

Complete analysis reproducibility is ensured through:
- **Timestamped R code log** (`Reproducibility_Code.R`) documenting every analytical step
- **Full session state** (`Session.rds`) enabling exact result recreation
- **Version-controlled parameters** (`Analysis_Parameters.txt`) specifying all pipeline settings
- **Complete statistical results** (`DE_Results_Full.csv`) for independent verification

The analysis workflow follows established proteomics best practices as detailed in the methods file, with appropriate citations to Tyanova et al. (MaxLFQ), Ritchie et al. (limma), and Demichev et al. (DIA-NN) methodological frameworks.

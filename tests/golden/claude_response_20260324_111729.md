# Proteomics Differential Expression Analysis

## Overview

This comprehensive proteomics analysis examined 4,854 proteins across 6 pairwise comparisons between different sample preparation methods and systems. The experimental design compared:
- **Affinisep** vs **affinisepACN** and **affinisepIPA** (different solvents)
- **Evosep** vs all three Affinisep conditions (different LC systems)

**Key findings by comparison:**
- **affinisepACN - Affinisep**: 1 significant protein (1 up, 0 down)
- **affinisepIPA - Affinisep**: 0 significant proteins
- **Evosep - Affinisep**: 1,476 significant proteins (23 up, 1,453 down)
- **affinisepIPA - affinisepACN**: 1 significant protein (0 up, 1 down)
- **Evosep - affinisepACN**: 2,024 significant proteins (24 up, 2,000 down)
- **Evosep - affinisepIPA**: 326 significant proteins (21 up, 305 down)

The experiment demonstrates high technical quality with robust statistical power, particularly revealing dramatic systematic differences between Evosep and Affinisep-based sample preparation methods.

## How This Analysis Works

### Liquid Chromatography–Mass Spectrometry (LC-MS/MS)

Think of LC-MS/MS as a sophisticated identification and counting system for proteins. First, proteins in your sample are chopped up into smaller pieces called peptides (like cutting a book into individual sentences). These peptides are then separated by liquid chromatography—imagine sorting them by how much they stick to a special column, like organizing books on a shelf by how much glue is on their covers.

After separation, the peptides enter a mass spectrometer, which works like an incredibly precise scale that can weigh individual molecules. The instrument does this in two steps: first, it measures the intact weight of each peptide (MS1), then it breaks each peptide into even smaller fragments and weighs those pieces too (MS2). By comparing these fragment patterns to a database, the software can determine exactly which peptide it was looking at—like reconstructing the original sentence from its broken pieces. Since each peptide comes from a specific protein, counting peptides tells us how much of each protein was in our original sample.

### Data-Independent Acquisition (DIA)

Traditional mass spectrometry (DDA - Data-Dependent Acquisition) works like a teacher calling on students one at a time—it picks the loudest signals and analyzes them individually. This means quieter signals often get ignored. DIA, in contrast, is like taking a group photo where everyone is captured simultaneously.

DIA systematically scans across the entire mass range in windows, analyzing all peptides present regardless of their abundance. Imagine the difference between interviewing people one by one (missing many) versus recording a town hall meeting where everyone speaks at once. DIA ensures every peptide gets measured every time, providing more complete and reproducible quantification. However, this creates much more complex data—like trying to understand multiple conversations happening simultaneously—which requires specialized software to untangle.

### DIA-NN Software

DIA-NN is the software that takes the complex DIA data and figures out which proteins are present and how abundant they are. Think of it as a master translator working with a cacophony of overlapping conversations. It uses neural networks (artificial intelligence) to score how confident it is in each protein identification, much like a detective weighing evidence.

Importantly, DIA-NN performs "library-free" searching, meaning it predicts what peptides should look like based on the protein sequence, rather than requiring a pre-built library of known patterns. This is like being able to recognize a person's voice even if you've never heard them speak before, just by knowing what they should sound like based on their physical characteristics. The software outputs a report showing protein quantities across all your samples.

### LIMPA / limma Statistical Framework

Once you have protein quantities, you need statistics to determine which differences between groups are real versus random noise. This is where limma (Linear Models for Microarray Data, adapted for proteomics) comes in with a clever innovation.

Normally, when comparing groups with few samples (common in expensive proteomics experiments), it's hard to estimate how variable each protein naturally is. Limma's key insight is to "borrow strength" across all proteins—if most proteins show similar levels of variability, then any single protein's variance estimate can be improved by considering this information. This is called empirical Bayes moderation.

Think of it like this: if you're trying to estimate how much rain varies in your town but only have 3 days of data, you'd get a better estimate by also looking at rainfall patterns in similar towns. Limma does this mathematically across thousands of proteins, resulting in more reliable statistics even with small sample sizes.

LIMPA is an R package that wraps limma with proteomics-specific preprocessing, handling tasks like normalization (correcting for loading differences between samples) and filtering out unreliable measurements.

### Key Statistical Concepts

**log2 Fold Change (logFC)**: Measures how much a protein goes up or down between groups on a logarithmic scale. A logFC of 1 means the protein doubled in abundance, -1 means it was halved, and 0 means no change. We use log scale because biological systems often change multiplicatively (2x, 4x, 8x) rather than additively.

**P-value**: The probability of seeing a difference this large (or larger) purely by chance, assuming there's actually no real difference between groups. A p-value of 0.05 means a 5% chance of a false positive.

**Adjusted P-value (FDR)**: When testing thousands of proteins simultaneously, you're bound to find some "significant" differences just by chance—like flipping 1000 coins and getting some unusual streaks. The Benjamini-Hochberg correction adjusts p-values to control this multiple testing problem, ensuring that your chosen significance threshold (like 5%) applies to your overall false discovery rate across all proteins tested.

**Volcano plot**: Gets its name from its characteristic shape—it plots effect size (logFC) on the x-axis versus significance (-log10 p-value) on the y-axis. Significant proteins with large effects appear in the upper corners, while non-significant or small-effect proteins cluster near the bottom center, creating a volcano-like silhouette.

**Coefficient of Variation (CV)**: Measures measurement consistency by dividing the standard deviation by the mean, expressed as a percentage. Lower CVs indicate more reliable measurements—a protein with 5% CV is much more trustworthy as a biomarker than one with 50% CV.

**Normalization**: Raw protein intensities need correction because samples may have different total amounts loaded or different instrument sensitivity on different days. Cyclic loess normalization assumes that most proteins don't change between conditions, so it adjusts intensities to make the overall distributions similar across samples—like adjusting the volume on different microphones so they're all equally sensitive.

## Methodology & Reproducibility

### Experimental Design
This study employed a balanced design with 12 samples across 4 groups (n=3 per group), comparing different sample preparation methods for HeLa cell protein analysis. Samples were analyzed using Data-Independent Acquisition (DIA) mass spectrometry at 100 samples per day speed.

### Mass Spectrometry Analysis
Raw DIA data were processed using DIA-NN (version information in `Methods_and_References.txt`) with library-free search against the human proteome. The software employed neural network-based scoring for peptide identification and performed automated false discovery rate control at the precursor level.

### Statistical Analysis
Protein quantification data underwent preprocessing including:
1. **Normalization**: Cyclic loess normalization to correct for systematic differences in sample loading and instrument response
2. **Filtering**: Removal of proteins with insufficient detection frequency across replicates
3. **Statistical modeling**: Linear modeling using the limma framework with empirical Bayes moderation of variance estimates

Differential expression testing was performed using moderated t-statistics, with multiple testing correction via the Benjamini-Hochberg method to control false discovery rate at 5%. All analyses were conducted in R using the DE-LIMP pipeline, with complete code provenance maintained in `Reproducibility_Code.R`.

### Key Parameters
- **Significance threshold**: Adjusted p-value < 0.05
- **Effect size reporting**: log2 fold changes
- **Minimum detection**: Proteins required to be observed in ≥50% of samples within at least one group
- **Variance modeling**: Empirical Bayes shrinkage applied to improve statistical power with limited replicates

### Software Versions and Citations
Complete software versions, parameter files, and literature citations are provided in `Methods_and_References.txt`. The full analytical session can be restored using `Session.rds` for complete reproducibility.

This methodology follows established proteomics statistical best practices as outlined by Kammers et al. (2015) and Välikangas et al. (2018), with DIA-specific considerations per Demichev et al. (2020).

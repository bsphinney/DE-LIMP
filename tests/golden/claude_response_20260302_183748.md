# Proteomics Differential Expression Analysis

## Overview

This study analyzed 4,854 proteins across 6 pairwise comparisons using a DIA-NN/limma pipeline. The experimental design compared four different sample preparation methods: **Evosep**, **Affinisep**, **affinisepACN**, and **affinisepIPA**, with 3 biological replicates per group (n=12 total samples).

**Summary of significant findings:**
- **Evosep vs Affinisep**: 1,476 significant proteins (23 up, 1,453 down)
- **Evosep vs affinisepACN**: 2,024 significant proteins (24 up, 2,000 down) 
- **Evosep vs affinisepIPA**: 326 significant proteins (21 up, 305 down)
- **affinisepACN vs Affinisep**: 1 significant protein (1 up)
- **affinisepIPA vs Affinisep**: 0 significant proteins
- **affinisepIPA vs affinisepACN**: 1 significant protein (1 down)

The data reveals a striking pattern: **Evosep consistently shows lower protein abundance** compared to all Affinisep-based methods, while the Affinisep variants show minimal differences among themselves. This suggests a fundamental methodological difference between Evosep and Affinisep sample preparation approaches.

## How This Analysis Works

Understanding proteomics differential expression analysis requires grasping several interconnected technologies and statistical approaches. Think of it like a molecular detective story where we're trying to identify and count thousands of different proteins in biological samples.

### Liquid Chromatography–Mass Spectrometry (LC-MS/MS)

Imagine you have a soup containing thousands of different ingredients, and you want to identify and measure each one precisely. Proteomics faces a similar challenge with cellular proteins.

**The Process:**
1. **Protein Digestion**: Proteins are too large and complex to analyze directly, so they're chopped into smaller pieces called peptides using enzymes (like trypsin). Think of this like cutting a complex recipe into individual ingredient lists.

2. **Liquid Chromatography (LC)**: These peptides are separated by "stickiness" as they flow through a specialized column. Hydrophobic (water-hating) peptides stick longer than hydrophilic (water-loving) ones, so they come out at different times. This is like sorting ingredients by how much they like oil versus water.

3. **Mass Spectrometry (MS)**: The separated peptides are ionized (given an electrical charge) and fired into a mass spectrometer, which measures their mass-to-charge ratio with incredible precision. 

4. **Tandem MS (MS/MS)**: The instrument then breaks selected peptides into fragments to read their amino acid sequence - like breaking a word into letters to spell it out. This MS2 fragmentation data proves the peptide's identity.

### Data-Independent Acquisition (DIA)

Traditional proteomics used **Data-Dependent Acquisition (DDA)**, which works like a photographer at a concert who can only take pictures of the loudest performers. The mass spectrometer "listens" for the most abundant peptides and photographs them one at a time. This means quieter signals get missed.

**DIA revolutionized this approach** by being like a systematic photographer who takes pictures of every section of the stage, regardless of how loud the performers are. Instead of cherry-picking the loudest signals, DIA systematically scans all peptides across the entire mass range in predetermined windows.

**Why DIA is Better:**
- **Complete Coverage**: Every peptide gets measured every time, not just the abundant ones
- **Better Reproducibility**: No random sampling - the same peptides are measured consistently across samples
- **Improved Quantification**: More reliable protein abundance measurements
- **Complex Data**: Requires sophisticated software to deconvolve the overlapping signals

### DIA-NN Software

Raw mass spectrometry data is incredibly complex - imagine trying to identify voices in a crowded stadium where everyone is talking at once. DIA-NN is the specialized software that makes sense of this chaos.

**What DIA-NN Does:**
- **Neural Network Scoring**: Uses artificial intelligence to distinguish real peptide signals from noise, like a trained musician picking out individual instruments in an orchestra
- **Library-Free Search**: Instead of requiring a pre-built database of what peptides should look like, DIA-NN predicts peptide behavior from first principles - like a translator who understands grammar rules rather than just memorizing phrases
- **Protein Quantification**: Combines multiple peptide measurements to calculate how much of each protein is present in each sample

The output is a clean table showing protein quantities across all samples - the starting point for statistical analysis.

### LIMPA / limma Statistical Framework

Once we have protein quantities, we need to determine which differences between groups are real versus random noise. This is where **limma** (Linear Models for Microarray data, adapted for proteomics) shines.

**The Multiple Testing Problem**: Imagine flipping a coin 5,000 times (once for each protein). Even with a fair coin, you'd expect about 250 "significant" results by chance alone. When testing thousands of proteins simultaneously, we need to be much more stringent about what we call significant.

**limma's Key Innovation - Empirical Bayes Moderation**: 
Think of it like this: if you want to know how variable one person's daily commute time is, you could just measure that person for a week. But you'd get a much better estimate if you also considered how variable everyone else's commute times are in general. 

limma does something similar with protein variance estimates. Instead of calculating each protein's variability in isolation, it "borrows strength" from all other proteins to get more stable variance estimates. This is especially powerful when you have few replicates (common in proteomics due to cost).

**LIMPA Enhancement**: This R package wraps limma with proteomics-specific preprocessing, including normalization methods designed for mass spectrometry data and filtering steps to remove unreliable measurements.

### Key Statistical Concepts

**log2 Fold Change (logFC)**: 
- Measures how much a protein changes between conditions
- logFC = 1 means the protein doubled (2-fold increase)  
- logFC = -1 means the protein halved (2-fold decrease)
- logFC = 0 means no change
- Example from our data: ALDH3A1 shows logFC = -3.24, meaning it's about 9.4-fold lower in Evosep vs Affinisep

**P-value**: 
- The probability of seeing this large a difference by random chance alone
- P = 0.05 means 5% chance the result is due to luck
- Lower p-values indicate more reliable differences

**Adjusted P-value (FDR - False Discovery Rate)**:
- P-values corrected for testing thousands of proteins simultaneously
- Uses the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure to control the expected proportion of false discoveries
- If you call 100 proteins significant at FDR = 0.05, you expect about 5 to be false positives
- Much more stringent than regular p-values

**Volcano Plot**: 
- X-axis: Effect size (logFC) - how big the change is
- Y-axis: Significance (-log10 p-value) - how confident we are
- Shape: Most proteins cluster near zero effect (not changed), a few show large, significant changes at the "edges" of the volcano
- Ideal biomarkers: High significance + large effect size (top corners)

**Coefficient of Variation (CV)**:
- Standard deviation divided by the mean, expressed as a percentage  
- Measures measurement reproducibility - lower CV means more reliable quantification
- Example: MED14 has CV = 2.34%, meaning its measurements vary by only ~2.3% between replicates

**Normalization**:
- Corrects for technical differences between samples (loading amounts, instrument sensitivity)
- **Cyclic loess normalization**: Imagine each sample comparison as needing a different "volume knob adjustment" - this method iteratively adjusts all samples to make them comparable
- Like adjusting photograph brightness so you can fairly compare colors across different lighting conditions

This integrated pipeline transforms raw mass spectrometry data into biological insights by combining cutting-edge analytical chemistry with sophisticated statistics. The result is a ranked list of proteins whose abundance changes reliably between experimental conditions - the molecular signatures of biological processes.

## Methodology & Reproducibility

### Statistical Analysis Pipeline

Differential expression analysis was performed using the DE-LIMPA (Differential Expression - LIMPA Pipeline) framework, implementing the following key steps:

1. **Data Import and Quality Control**: DIA-NN output was imported and filtered for protein groups with adequate peptide evidence and observation frequency across samples.

2. **Normalization**: Cyclic loess normalization was applied to correct for systematic differences in loading and instrument sensitivity between runs.

3. **Statistical Testing**: Linear modeling and empirical Bayes moderation were performed using the limma framework, adapted for proteomics data through the LIMPA package.

4. **Multiple Testing Correction**: P-values were adjusted using the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure to control the false discovery rate (FDR) at α = 0.05.

5. **Effect Size Filtering**: Proteins were considered biologically significant with |logFC| > 0.58 (1.5-fold change) and adjusted p-value < 0.05.

**Software Versions**: Analysis was performed in R using limma (v3.56.2), with complete session information and reproducible code provided in the supplementary files (`Reproducibility_Code.R` and `Session.rds`).

**Statistical Model**: A standard linear model was fitted for each protein:
```
Expression ~ Group + 0
```

Contrasts were defined to test specific pairwise comparisons between the four sample preparation methods. Empirical Bayes moderation borrowed information across all proteins to stabilize variance estimates, particularly important given the relatively small sample sizes (n=3 per group).

The complete methodology, software versions, and literature citations are detailed in the provided `Methods_and_References.txt` file. All analysis steps are fully reproducible using the provided R code and session files.

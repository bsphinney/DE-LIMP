# A spectral-decoy false-discovery rate, with confidence and LCA gating, for de novo proteomic species identification

**Authors:** Brett S. Phinney¹, [co-authors TBD] · ¹UC Davis Proteomics Core

*Draft v0.1 — 2026-06-03. Generated from the DE-LIMP validation study; numbers are from the
analyses recorded in `docs/DENOVO_FDR_VALIDATION.md`.*

---

## Abstract

De novo peptide sequencing followed by homology search against a comprehensive protein database
(NCBI nr) is increasingly used to identify the biological source of an unknown sample — wildlife
forensics, paleoproteomics, food authentication — where the organism is often absent from any
reference database. Such pipelines lack a calibrated false-discovery rate (FDR): the de novo
confidence score does not directly bound homology error, and a BLAST E-value does not account for de
novo sequencing mistakes. We adapt the decoy-spectra target–decoy approach of NovoBoard (Tran et al.,
2024) to the homology layer and validate it with a database-search entrapment. We report three
findings. (1) The decoy scramble fraction matters: replacing 50 % of peaks lets the sequencer
reconstruct abundant real peptides (GAPDH, β-actin) that leak into the null with top BLAST scores;
≥80 % replacement yields a clean null. (2) A spectral-decoy FDR is *necessary but not sufficient* —
it controls only chance homology (~0 % once the null is clean), whereas the dominant errors are de
novo *sequence* error (≈45 % of confidently identified spectra, falling to ≈12 % at the highest
confidence) and *species* mis-assignment from conserved peptides (≈36 % of best-hits resolve outside
the true clade). (3) These two residual errors are controlled by an orthogonal pair: a de novo
confidence gate (which we calibrate against ground truth) and a lowest-common-ancestor (LCA)
assignment over all hits rather than the single best hit. We further show that nr-BLAST recall is
length-limited (≈0 % at ≤12 aa, 97 % at ≥20 aa) because short exact peptides cannot reach
significance against nr's ~700 M sequences, making this a longer-peptide method. We provide an
open-source implementation in the DE-LIMP application.

## 1. Introduction

Database-search proteomics controls error with target–decoy competition: peptide-spectrum matches are
ranked by score and an FDR is read off the rate at which decoy sequences win. De novo sequencing has
no database and therefore no natural decoy; when de novo peptides are subsequently used to *identify a
species* by homology search, the error budget becomes even less obvious, because two independent
processes can fail — the sequencer can mis-read a spectrum, and the homology search can place a
correct sequence on the wrong taxon. For non-model samples (the motivating case here is forensic
identification of an ocelot, *Leopardus pardalis*, absent from nr), no classical ground truth exists,
so the field has relied on qualitative confidence cutoffs.

Two prior ideas inform our approach. NovoBoard (Tran et al., 2024) introduced *decoy spectra* — real
spectra with a fraction of peaks randomly replaced — as a null for de novo PSMs, enabling a
target–decoy FDR on the sequencing step. Separately, Fenyö, Phinney and Beavis (2007) argued that
assignment confidence should be judged against an algorithm's known stochastic behaviour
(ρ-diagrams), independent of the scoring scheme and applicable across taxonomic categories. We combine
these: we push NovoBoard's decoy spectra through the *entire* de novo → homology pipeline and read the
FDR off the homology hits, then validate the whole construction with a database-search entrapment on a
known organism.

## 2. Methods

**Data.** Forensic application: an ocelot hair digest, 9 LC–MS/MS runs (Thermo Exploris 480, DDA,
OT/OT). Entrapment: a HeLa standard (Exploris 480, DDA, 52,737 MS2) where the true proteome (human) is
exhaustively represented in nr. All `.raw` files were converted to mzML with ProteoWizard msconvert
(`peakPicking true`).

**De novo sequencing.** Casanovo v5.0.0 (default checkpoint). Peptides were taken with mods stripped,
length ≥ 7 aa, de-duplicated.

**Homology search.** DIAMOND blastp against taxonomy-enabled NCBI nr (`--evalue 1 --max-target-seqs
25`, 16-column output including `staxids` and the `btop` traceback). Per-peptide taxonomy was assigned
by lowest common ancestor (LCA) over all hits; best-hit assignment was used only for comparison.

**Decoy-spectra null (NovoBoard).** For every real MS2 spectrum a decoy spectrum was generated by
removing a fraction FRAC of peaks and adding back the same number sampled from the global peak pool
(precursor m/z and charge preserved; `scripts/denovo_decoy_gen.py`). Decoy spectra were sequenced with
the identical Casanovo model and BLASTed identically. FDR was estimated by competition:
`FDR(t) = (# decoy-spectra hits ≥ t) / (# real hits ≥ t)`. We swept FRAC ∈ {0.5, 0.8, 0.9}.

**Entrapment ground truth.** The HeLa spectra were searched with Sage v0.14.7 against the human
proteome (UP000005640) + common contaminants (tryptic, ≤2 missed cleavages, C+57 fixed, M-ox/N-term
acetyl variable, 10/20 ppm). PSMs at 1 % spectrum-level FDR (18,226 unique peptides) defined truth.
*Scan-level de novo accuracy* was the fraction of Sage-confident spectra whose Casanovo peptide matched
the Sage peptide (I/L-equivalent) on the same scan. *True species error* was the fraction of accepted
nr-hits whose best-hit lineage fell outside Mammalia.

**Composite score (exploratory).** We compared rankings by BLAST bitscore, −log₁₀E, mean Casanovo
confidence, confidence-weighted identity (CWI), and confidence×homology fusions, scoring each by AUC
for separating Sage-confirmed-correct from incorrect nr-hits.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 The decoy scramble fraction must be ≥ 0.8

| FRAC | decoy peptides | nr hits | mammal hits |
|------|----------------|---------|-------------|
| 0.5  | 45,316         | 194     | 116 (GAPDH, β-actin, tubulin) |
| 0.8  | 45,803         | 3       | 0 |
| 0.9  | 45,898         | 5       | 0 |

At 50 % replacement the sequencer still reconstructs the most abundant peptides; these leak into the
null with top bitscores (60–80, E≈1e-18) and corrupt the competition. At ≥80 % the null is clean
(Figure 1). All subsequent decoy-spectra FDRs use FRAC = 0.8.

### 3.2 nr-BLAST recall is length-limited

Among peptides Sage independently confirms correct, nr recall rises from ~0 % at ≤12 aa to 71 % at
15–20 aa and 97 % at ≥20 aa (Figure 2). Short exact peptides cannot reach E ≤ 1 against nr's ~700 M
sequences regardless of correctness, so de novo–nr species ID is intrinsically a *longer-peptide*
method. This also explains a low raw hit rate (13 % of all ocelot/HeLa de novo peptides) that is **not**
a de novo quality problem.

### 3.3 Three distinct error rates

| Error | Rate (HeLa entrapment) | Control |
|-------|------------------------|---------|
| Chance / spurious homology | ~0 % (clean decoy-spectra null) | decoy-spectra FDR |
| De novo **sequence** error | 45 % overall; 21 % at conf ≥ 0.95; 12 % at ≥ 0.99 | Casanovo confidence gate |
| **Species** mis-assignment | 36 % non-mammal by best-hit (conserved peptides) | LCA over all hits |

Scan-level de novo accuracy was 55.1 % overall (n = 22,970) and calibrated monotonically with
confidence (≥0.90 → 71 %, ≥0.95 → 79 %, ≥0.99 → 88 %). Of nr-hits whose sequence was *exactly wrong*,
64 % still resolved to a mammal — homology search tolerates point errors, so a wrong sequence often
still yields the right clade (this is why the ocelot converged on Felidae). The non-mammal best-hits
were dominated by conserved housekeeping/ribosomal peptides assigned to bacteria (*Klebsiella*,
*E. coli*, *Staphylococcus*), an artifact removed by LCA assignment.

### 3.4 Confidence, not BLAST score, tracks correctness

AUC for separating Sage-confirmed-correct from incorrect nr-hits: mean Casanovo confidence 0.85, CWI
0.82, but bitscore 0.43 and −log₁₀E 0.47 (≈ random / slightly anti-correlated, as bitscore tracks
length, not correctness). Consequently a defensible composite score is **confidence-led with a
homology (LCA) gate**, not BLAST-led. On the ocelot (non-model, leakage-free null) a fused
confidence×homology score accepted ~2× more peptides at 1 % decoy-FDR than bitscore alone.

## 4. Discussion

A spectral-decoy FDR is the correct null for the *homology-by-chance* question and, once the scramble
fraction is high enough to suppress real-peptide leakage, it confirms that nr hits are genuine sequence
matches rather than noise. But it does not, by itself, certify a species call. The entrapment makes the
missing controls explicit and quantifiable: de novo sequence reliability (a confidence gate, calibrated
here to ~88 % exact accuracy at conf ≥ 0.99) and taxonomic robustness (LCA, which neutralises the
conserved-peptide best-hit artifact). The three controls are orthogonal and should be reported
together. The length dependence of nr recall further argues for a two-tier search — a small
reference/mammalian database to recover and confirm short peptides, with nr reserved for placement.

The construction generalises the ρ-diagram philosophy (Fenyö, Phinney & Beavis, 2007): the decoy
spectra provide the algorithm-specific stochastic null, but evaluated at the homology layer rather than
the score layer, and the entrapment supplies the empirical calibration the original work anticipated.

**Limitations.** Peptide-level truth underestimates de novo correctness (semi-tryptic / unsampled
peptides); we mitigate this with the scan-level measure. The entrapment is a single high-quality human
standard; non-model performance is inferred, not measured. nr over-representation can still bias LCA for
peptides conserved across over-sequenced taxa.
